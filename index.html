Network Protocol Consensus

Network Contribution Rewards

In the network protocol, Proof of Transport, Stake/Store, and Computation/Work are pivotal mechanisms that dynamically reward contributing members. Proof of Transport ensures that nodes actively participating in data transfer are recognized and rewarded, validating their contribution to network connectivity and stability. Stake/Store incentivizes nodes to allocate their storage resources, creating a distributed data repository that enhances network reliability. By staking their resources, nodes earn rewards based on the amount and duration of their contribution, promoting a stable and robust network infrastructure. Computation/Work recognizes nodes providing computational power, ensuring that processing tasks are distributed and managed efficiently across the network.

Fairness and transparency are core principles embedded in these mechanisms. The network protocol employs a decentralized ledger to record and verify contributions, ensuring that all actions are transparent and verifiable by any network participant. Rewards are calculated based on predefined algorithms that objectively measure the value of each contribution, whether it's transport, storage, or computation. This approach guarantees that all members are fairly compensated for their efforts, fostering trust and encouraging ongoing participation. By transparently managing contributions and rewards, the network maintains its integrity and promotes equitable resource distribution.

Day/Night utilization

Network Elasticity

Network Elasticity is a key feature of this system, where individuals contributing their computing, transport, and storage resources are incentivized for facilitating their capacity. The network dynamically adjusts to varying demands, utilizing between 0% to 100% of a node's capacity based on current needs. This flexibility ensures optimal performance and resource utilization, allowing the network to scale efficiently. Node owners can throttle their contributions as needed, providing the necessary resources for their own tasks while still supporting the network.

For instance, during the day, a node owner might throttle 40% of their capacity, retaining 60% for personal use. At night, the situation might reverse, with the node contributing up to 90% of its capacity to the network. This dynamic adjustment ensures that the network can leverage available resources effectively, while still respecting the needs of individual contributors. By incentivizing participation, the system encourages a robust and resilient infrastructure, capable of scaling and adapting to meet varying demands.

Components

Example Network

Network Scalability

Using the three base components of Satellite (Backbone), Service Nodes, and Distribution Nodes, a complete network can be built, scalable from small to large deployments. These components form the backbone of a robust and versatile publishing infrastructure. The Satellite (Backbone) serves as the central hub, providing primary connectivity and routing, while Service Nodes act as intermediaries that can conceal complex networks. Distribution Nodes, accessed either through load balancers or directly via Wi-Fi, serve as the endpoints, ensuring that services are effectively delivered to the end-users.

By employing a specialized protocol, these types of nodes are automatically configured and integrated into the network they are intended to serve. This protocol ensures seamless connectivity and efficient communication among all components, regardless of the network's scale. Each participant in the network can function as a potential failover component, enhancing redundancy and reliability. This decentralized approach allows the network to grow organically, adapting to increasing demands and complexities without the need for extensive manual configuration.

Area Coverage

Network Failover

In the event of a general outage or disaster, failover is built into the network protocol to provide multiple options for finding redundant components without any complex configuration. Due to the decentralized nature of this network, every participant becomes a potential failover component.

With the OnLive project definition (active run in the ESP Lab), disaster recovery is managed by collective participants with mobile phones who are within range or near service or distribution nodes. In the event of a major service failure, a grid of phones in the vicinity automatically forms a network, replacing the failed network components with this grid of phones.

By sending an alert to the phones in the area requesting support through the activation of the OnLive DeckPack applications, the network is re-established. Thank you, citizens.
